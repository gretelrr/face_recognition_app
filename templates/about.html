What is prosopagnosia?

Prosopagnosia, also called ‚Äúface blindness‚Äù, is an impaired ability to recognise individuals by their face, categorised as developmental (onset in early childhood) and acquired (onset later in life). [1] For both acquired and developmental prosopagnosia, standardised criteria is to a large extent absent [2], often resulting in misdiagnosis, being confused with visual agnosia, memory deficits, or inattentiveness. [3] [4]

How many are affected by prosopagnosia?

Developmental prosopagnosia affects approximately 2 - 2,5% of the population and seems to sometimes occur hereditarily. [5] More recent studies have found prosopagnosia occurring even more often, claiming it may affect over 3% of the population. [6]

Social challenges of prosopagnosia

Adults with developmental prosopagnosia have reported recurrent and sometimes traumatic social interaction difficulties, such as failing to recognise close friends, colleagues, and family members, leading them to chronic anxiety, embarrassment, guilt, and social avoidance [7]
